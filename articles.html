Life Is Not Fair — And That Is Okay

Life distributes advantages unevenly. Some are born into royalty, others into poverty. Some inherit health, others inherit illness. Some are raised in environments that cultivate discipline, others are raised in chaos. The starting lines are never equal.

And yet, the conversation about fairness often stops at the problem. We acknowledge the imbalance, feel its weight, and then — consciously or not — use it as an explanation for where we are. That's where the danger lies.

The Cost of Expecting Fairness

When you expect life to be fair, you set yourself up for a specific kind of suffering. Every setback feels like an injustice. Every advantage someone else has becomes a reason your progress is slower. The framework of fairness turns the world into a court where you are perpetually the wronged party.

This doesn't mean injustice doesn't exist. It does. But anchoring your identity to what you didn't receive — rather than what you choose to do — is a slow form of paralysis.

What You Can Control

The stoics understood this well. The dichotomy of control — separating what is within your power from what is not — is not a resignation. It is a focusing mechanism. You stop spending energy on what you cannot change and redirect it toward what you can.

Your inputs. Your consistency. Your decisions under pressure. Your willingness to build when building is difficult.

The Real Question

The question is never whether your circumstances are fair. The question is what you build from those circumstances. History is full of people who built remarkable things from unfavorable conditions — not because the odds flipped, but because they worked as if the odds were irrelevant.

Accepting that life is not fair is not pessimism. It is clarity. And from clarity, you can move.

Identity-Driven Habits vs Outcome-Driven Habits

Why do we set New Year's resolutions but fail to uphold them? The answer lies in understanding the difference between identity-driven and outcome-driven habits — and why the former almost always wins.

Outcome-Driven Habits

Most people build habits around outcomes. "I want to lose 10kg." "I want to read 24 books." "I want to earn more money." The goal is the destination. The habit is just a vehicle.

The problem: when you miss a day, the system collapses. Because the goal was never tied to who you are — it was tied to what you wanted to have. One skip, and the motivation evaporates.

Identity-Driven Habits

Identity-driven habits start with a different question: not "what do I want?" but "who am I becoming?" When you define yourself as a reader, missing a day feels like a violation of your identity — not just a missed target. The habit becomes self-reinforcing.

James Clear framed it well in Atomic Habits: every action is a vote for the kind of person you want to become. The goal is not to run a marathon — it is to become a runner. The marathon follows.

How to Apply This

Start by asking: what is the identity behind the habit I want to build? Replace "I want to code every day" with "I am a developer. Developers code." Small reframe. Significant difference in follow-through.

The habits that stick are the ones that feel like expressions of who you are — not obligations imposed by who you want to become.

The Impact of Artificial Intelligence

AI is no longer a concept from science fiction. It is a tool reshaping how we work, learn, communicate, and think. For anyone building a future in tech, understanding its trajectory is not optional — it is foundational.

Automation and the Labor Market

The most visible effect of AI is on repetitive, pattern-based work. Administrative tasks, data entry, customer service scripts — these are being automated at scale. The jobs that remain will require what machines struggle to replicate: creativity, judgment, and contextual reasoning.

This creates pressure on anyone who isn't actively building higher-order skills. But it also creates opportunity — tools that once required entire teams can now be operated by individuals who know how to use them well.

AI as a Learning Tool

One of the most underrated shifts is how AI changes learning. A student who learns how to use AI effectively — to break down concepts, generate examples, and identify gaps — has access to a form of personalized tutoring that was previously available only to the privileged few.

The question is not whether to use these tools. It is whether to use them passively (letting AI think for you) or actively (using AI to accelerate your own thinking).

Where This Leaves You

AI is not a threat if you are building. The people most at risk are those waiting for the world to stay the same. The people positioned well are those who treat AI as a lever — something that amplifies what they are already building, thinking, and creating.

Build. Document. Adapt. That's the only coherent response to a rapidly changing landscape.
